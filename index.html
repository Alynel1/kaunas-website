Discover the second biggest city of Lithuania

General Presentation

Kaunas is the second largest city in Lithuania. Located at the confluence of the Neman and Neris rivers, Kaunas has served multiple times as the temporary capital of the country. Today, it has around 300,000 inhabitants and covers an area of 157 km². Kaunas is known for its vibrant culture, rich history, and unique architectural blend.

The History of Kaunas

Founded in the 14th century, Kaunas became an important commercial hub in the region. It has been occupied by various powers, including Russians, Germans, and Soviets. Between the two World Wars, Kaunas served as the temporary capital of Lithuania. Its history is marked by cultural resistance and national pride.

Monuments and Points of Interest

Laisvės Alėja (Liberty Avenue): The main pedestrian street of Kaunas, filled with shops, cafes, and cultural landmarks. It stretches over 1.6 kilometers and is one of the longest pedestrian streets in Eastern Europe.

St. Michael the Archangel Church: A striking Neo-Byzantine church located at the end of Liberty Avenue. Built in the 19th century, it is a powerful symbol of Kaunas' historical resilience.

Kaunas Basilica (Cathedral of St. Peter and St. Paul): The largest Gothic church in Lithuania, located in the Old Town. Its origins date back to the 15th century.

Vytautas the Great Church: One of the oldest brick Gothic churches in Lithuania, located near the Nemunas River. It was built for the Franciscan monks in the 15th century.

Kaunas is famous for its diverse architecture. From Gothic churches to Baroque façades and modernist interwar buildings, the city reflects centuries of European artistic and cultural development.
